# Certificate of Analysis (COA) FAQ

## (1) General Information

### Q1: What is a Certificate of Analysis (COA) for research peptides?
**A:** A Certificate of Analysis (COA) is an official quality document issued by the manufacturer's Quality Control (QC) department that reports the results of analytical testing performed on a **specific batch** of peptide product. It certifies that the batch has been tested against established specifications and meets the required quality standards.

Each COA includes:
- Product information (name, catalog number)
- Batch/lot number for traceability
- Test results for each quality parameter (appearance, purity, identity, content, etc.)
- Specification limits and measured values
- The HPLC chromatogram and LC-MS spectrum as supporting evidence
- Authorized QC signatures

A COA is batch-specific — it applies only to the exact batch it represents, not to the product in general.

### Q2: What is the difference between a COA, a TDS, and an SDS?
**A:** These three documents serve different purposes and are often confused:

| Document | Purpose | Batch-Specific? | Content |
|----------|---------|:---------------:|---------|
| **COA** (Certificate of Analysis) | Reports actual test results for a specific batch | Yes | Purity, identity, assay, water content, endotoxin, HPLC and MS data |
| **TDS** (Technical Data Sheet) | Provides general product specifications, handling, and storage information | No | Chemical properties, solubility, general specifications, analytical methods |
| **SDS** (Safety Data Sheet) | Provides safety, hazard, handling, and emergency information | No | GHS hazard classifications, PPE, first aid, toxicological data |

Think of it this way: the TDS tells you the **target specifications** for a product, the COA tells you what the **actual batch** achieved, and the SDS tells you how to **handle it safely**.

### Q3: Should every peptide shipment include a COA?
**A:** Yes. Every shipment of research peptides should be accompanied by a **batch-specific COA** that corresponds to the exact batch number printed on the product vial label. A COA is not a generic or template document — each batch receives its own QC testing and its own COA.

If a supplier does not provide a COA, or provides only a generic (non-batch-specific) document, this is a significant quality concern. It may indicate that:
- The product was not independently tested
- The supplier cannot trace the product to its QC records
- The purity claim is based on the supplier's general process, not actual analysis

RPL Peptide provides a batch-specific COA with every shipment, including full analytical data.

### Q4: What should I verify first when I receive a COA?
**A:** When you receive a COA, follow this priority checklist:

1. **Batch number match:** Confirm the batch/lot number on the COA matches the batch number printed on the product vial label. If these do not match, do not use the product.
2. **Product name match:** Verify the product name and catalog number correspond to what was ordered.
3. **Purity specification:** Check that the reported purity meets or exceeds the stated minimum (e.g., ≥98%).
4. **Identity confirmation:** Verify the LC-MS result confirms the expected molecular weight within ±0.5 Da.
5. **Analysis date:** Confirm the analysis was performed recently (within 6 months).
6. **All results conform:** Ensure all tested parameters are marked as passing their specifications.
7. **QC signatures:** Verify the COA is signed or authorized by the QC department.

## (2) Reading Your COA

### Q5: What are all the sections of a typical peptide COA?
**A:** A comprehensive peptide COA is organized into distinct sections:

**Section 1 — Header Information:**
- Supplier name and contact details
- COA document number
- Product name, catalog number, and batch/lot number
- Quantity tested and packaging information
- Analysis date, manufacture date, and expiry/retest date

**Section 2 — Analytical Results Table:**
- Each test parameter listed with its specification, result, method reference, and pass/fail status

| Parameter Example | Specification | Result | Method | Status |
|-------------------|:------------:|:------:|:------:|:------:|
| Appearance | White to off-white powder | White powder | Visual | Conforms |
| HPLC Purity | ≥98.0% | 98.7% | RP-HPLC 214 nm | Conforms |
| Identity (LC-MS) | ±0.5 Da | +0.2 Da | ESI-TOF | Conforms |
| Peptide Content | 70–90% | 84.5% | UV 280 nm | Conforms |
| Water Content | <5.0% | 2.1% | Karl Fischer | Conforms |
| Endotoxin | <5.0 EU/mg | <1.0 EU/mg | Kinetic LAL | Conforms |

**Section 3 — System Suitability Data:**
- Theoretical plates, tailing factor, retention time RSD
- Confirms the HPLC system was performing correctly

**Section 4 — Certifying Signatures:**
- Analyst, Reviewer, and QA Approver signatures or electronic equivalents
- Authorization date

**Section 5 — Supporting Documents (attached):**
- Full HPLC chromatogram with integration markings
- LC-MS mass spectrum (raw and deconvoluted)

### Q6: What does "peptide content" mean on a COA and why does it matter?
**A:** Peptide content (also called assay) is the percentage of the total powder mass that is actual peptide, as opposed to non-peptide components such as water, counter-ions, and residual solvents.

**What it tells you:**
- A peptide content of 82.3% means that 82.3% of the total mass is peptide material
- The remaining 17.7% consists of water (~2–5%), TFA or acetate counter-ion (~5–10%), and trace residual solvents

**Why it matters for dosing:**
If a vial is labeled as containing 5 mg of peptide but the content is only 80%, you actually have approximately **4 mg** of peptide-related material (some of which is impurities per HPLC purity). This directly impacts your concentration calculations.

**How to interpret both numbers together:**

| COA Data | Value | What It Means |
|----------|:-----:|---------------|
| HPLC purity | 98.5% | 98.5% of peptide material is target |
| Peptide content | 82.0% | 82% of total mass is peptide material |
| Effective target peptide | 80.8% | 98.5% × 82.0% = 80.8% of total mass |

Always distinguish between HPLC purity and peptide content — they measure fundamentally different attributes.

### Q7: How do I distinguish between purity and content numbers on a COA?
**A:** Look for these tells on the COA to distinguish them:

| Label Used | What It Measures | Typical Value |
|------------|-----------------|:-------------:|
| "HPLC Purity" / "Purity (HPLC)" / "Area %" | HPLC area % purity at 214 nm | ≥98% |
| "Peptide Content" / "Assay" / "Content (w/w)" | Mass % of peptide in total powder | 70–90% |
| "Purity (Anhydrous)" | Purity corrected for water | ≥98% (higher than regular content) |
| "Purity (Solvent-Free)" | Purity corrected for solvents | Similar to HPLC purity |
| "Net Peptide Purity" | HPLC purity × Peptide content | 70–90% (not commonly reported) |

If the COA only reports a single number labeled "Purity," it is almost certainly HPLC area % purity. When in doubt, ask the supplier to clarify.

### Q8: What does the HPLC chromatogram on the COA tell me?
**A:** The HPLC chromatogram provides visual evidence of the product's purity profile. Key things to examine:

- **Main peak shape:** Should be symmetrical and Gaussian-like. A sharp, clean main peak with minimal tailing indicates good chromatographic performance.
- **Impurity peaks:** Look for additional peaks besides the main one. Their number, size, and position tell you about the impurity profile.
- **Baseline:** Should be flat and stable before, during, and after the main peak.
- **Resolution:** Impurity peaks should be clearly separated from the main peak (resolution ≥1.5).
- **Integration markings:** Proper chromatograms show where the baseline starts and ends under each peak.

A clean chromatogram with one dominant peak and only trace-level impurity peaks is what you want to see. Additional peaks suggest the presence of related substances. The absence of any impurity peaks is suspicious for most peptides.

### Q9: What does the LC-MS spectrum on the COA confirm?
**A:** The LC-MS spectrum confirms the **identity** of the peptide. Specifically, it confirms that the molecular weight of the main peak matches the theoretical mass calculated from the peptide's amino acid sequence.

On the LC-MS spectrum, verify:

1. **Deconvoluted mass:** The single calculated neutral mass should match the theoretical mass within ±0.5 Da.
2. **Charge state envelope:** For ESI spectra, a clean series of multiply charged peaks indicates good ionization and sample quality.
3. **No unexpected masses:** The presence of additional deconvoluted mass peaks indicates a mixture.
4. **Adduct profile:** Small sodium (+22 Da) or potassium (+38 Da) adducts are acceptable; large adduct peaks suggest contamination.

For example, for BPC-157 (C₆₂H₉₈N₁₆O₂₂, theoretical MW = 1419.53 Da), an observed deconvoluted mass of 1419.69 Da (+0.16 Da) is well within the ±0.5 Da specification and confirms identity.

## (3) Batch Traceability

### Q10: What is a batch number and how does traceability work?
**A:** A **batch number** (also called lot number) is a unique alphanumeric identifier assigned to a specific production batch. It links the physical product in your hand to its complete quality record:

- **Forward traceability:** From raw materials → synthesis → purification → QC testing → COA → distribution
- **Backward traceability:** From the product vial → batch number → QC records → production parameters → raw material certificates

RPL Peptide batch numbers follow a structured format (internal SOP) that encodes production date, product code, and sequence number, ensuring each batch has a unique identifier.

**Critical rule:** The batch number on the COA must match the batch number on the product vial label. If they differ, the traceability chain is broken.

### Q11: How can I verify a COA using QR codes or batch lookup?
**A:** RPL Peptide provides **QR codes** on COAs that link directly to the batch-specific quality documentation. To verify a COA:

1. **Scan the QR code** with a smartphone or QR reader — this links to a verification page showing the batch's test data
2. **Compare the online data** with the printed COA — they should match exactly
3. **Check the batch number** on the product vial matches both the COA and the online record

If a QR code is not available:
- Request the COA directly from the supplier's QC department with matching batch numbers
- Verify all analytical data is consistent (purity, identity, appearance)
- Compare with previous COAs from the same product for consistency

### Q12: What does the retest date mean on a COA?
**A:** The **retest date** (also called reanalysis date) is the date after which the batch should be re-tested to confirm it still meets specifications. It is distinct from an **expiry date**:

| Term | Meaning | Basis |
|------|---------|-------|
| **Expiry date** | The date after which the product should not be used | Stability studies demonstrate degradation beyond spec |
| **Retest date** | The date at which the batch should be re-tested to confirm it still meets specifications | The manufacturer's confidence period based on storage conditions |

For research peptides, retest dates are more common than expiry dates because:
- Stability data for each peptide may be limited
- Proper storage (−20°C, desiccated, protected from light) extends usable life
- A retest confirms suitability rather than assuming degradation

If your vial is past the retest date, you may still use it after verifying:
- The product has been stored continuously at −20°C
- Visual inspection shows no change (clumping, discoloration)
- Ideally, HPLC re-analysis confirms purity is still within specification

## (4) Troubleshooting

### Q13: What if the batch number on my vial doesn't match the COA?
**A:** A batch number mismatch between the vial and COA is a **serious quality system failure**. Do not use the product. Take these steps:

1. **Contact the supplier immediately** with photos of both the vial label and the COA
2. **Request clarification** — the correct COA for your batch, or an explanation
3. **Do not reconstitute or use the product** until the issue is resolved
4. If a corrected COA is provided, verify the batch numbers now match

A mismatch indicates either:
- The wrong COA was included with your shipment (documentation error)
- The product was mislabeled (quality failure)
- The traceability system is compromised

Reputable suppliers will treat this as a high-priority issue and resolve it promptly.

### Q14: What if a COA shows results that don't meet specifications?
**A:** If any test result on the COA fails to meet its specification, the batch should **not** have been released for distribution. Steps to take:

1. **Double-check the spec vs. result:** Confirm you are reading the correct specification for that parameter. For example, "≥98%" is the minimum, so 97.8% is a failure.
2. **Contact the supplier's QC department** with the batch number and specific parameter of concern
3. **Request investigation** — good suppliers will investigate and explain
4. **Request replacement** or a different batch that meets specifications

Common scenarios:
| Observation | Likely Cause |
|-------------|--------------|
| Purity slightly below spec (e.g., 97.5%) | Purification yield/cut point was prioritized over purity |
| Water content above spec | Inadequate lyophilization or package seal failure |
| Mass off by >0.5 Da | Sequence error during synthesis |

RPL Peptide maintains release specifications that are strictly enforced — batches that do not meet all specifications are not shipped.

### Q15: What are the red flags that indicate a COA may be unreliable?
**A:** Watch for these warning signs when evaluating a COA:

**Documentation red flags:**
- No batch number or a generic batch number (e.g., "001")
- No HPLC chromatogram attached
- No LC-MS mass spectrum attached
- Only one purity number reported with no supporting data
- No signatures or authorization markings
- COA looks like a photocopy of a photocopy (poor quality reproduction)
- The COA is a "template" with blank batch-specific fields
- No manufacturer information or quality department contact

**Data red flags:**
- Purity ≥99.5% for a complex, long-sequence peptide (suspicious)
- Peptide content >95% (impossible — must account for water and counter-ion)
- HPLC at 280 nm only (misses most impurities; only detects aromatic residues)
- No water content data (cannot assess lyophilization quality)
- No LC-MS identity data (identity not confirmed)
- Exact same purity value on every batch (e.g., always 98.00%)
- Suspiciously clean chromatogram with zero visible impurity peaks

**Numerical red flags:**
- "100% pure" — scientifically impossible
- "Peptide content 99%" — ignores water and counter-ion
- Purity stated without method or wavelength specified

### Q16: How should I retain and manage COAs for my research records?
**A:** Proper COA management is important for research documentation and audit readiness:

**Best practices:**
1. **Store COAs electronically** as PDF files in a dedicated folder organized by product name and batch number
2. **Match each COA to its physical vial** — note the batch number on your lab records
3. **Retain COAs** for the duration of the research project plus at least 3–5 years (or per your institution's policy)
4. **Keep the chain of documentation** — if you repackage a product, document the original batch number and COA reference
5. **Archive previous versions** if a COA is revised or corrected

**What to store with each COA:**
- The product purchase order or invoice number
- Date received and storage location
- Any re-analysis or retest results if performed
- Notes on experimental use (which experiments used which batch)

RPL Peptide provides electronic COAs (PDF) with every shipment for easy digital record-keeping.
