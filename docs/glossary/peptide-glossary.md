## Introduction

This glossary defines key terms used in peptide research, synthesis, analysis, and quality control. It is designed as a quick reference for researchers, procurement professionals, and quality assurance teams. Terms are organized alphabetically with cross-references to related entries.

For deeper technical information, refer to our white paper library at https://rplpeptides.com/white-papers and FAQ knowledge base at https://rplpeptides.com/knowledge-base.

---

## A

### Acetylation
The addition of an acetyl group (CH₃CO—) to the N-terminus of a peptide. Acetylation neutralizes the positive charge of the N-terminal amine, improving stability and mimicking natural proteins that are often N-terminally acetylated.

### ACN (Acetonitrile)
An organic solvent used as the mobile phase in reversed-phase HPLC peptide analysis. ACN concentration is typically increased during the gradient to elute peptides based on hydrophobicity.

### Aib (α-Aminoisobutyric Acid)
A non-natural amino acid used to induce helical structure and provide resistance to enzymatic degradation. Used in several GLP-1 analogs including Semaglutide, Tirzepatide, and Retatrutide to confer DPP-4 resistance at position 8.

### Aliquot
A portion of a larger sample divided into separate containers for single-use storage. Proper aliquoting prevents degradation from repeated freeze-thaw cycles. Most reconstituted peptides should be aliquoted before freezing at -20°C.

### Amidation
The addition of an amide group (—CONH₂) to the C-terminus of a peptide. C-terminal amidation is common in naturally occurring peptides and often enhances stability and biological activity compared to the free acid form.

### Amino Acid
The building block of peptides and proteins. The 20 standard amino acids share a common backbone structure (amino group, carboxyl group, side chain) but differ in their side chains. Peptide synthesis can also incorporate hundreds of non-natural amino acids.

### AMP (Antimicrobial Peptide)
Naturally occurring short peptides that kill or inhibit microorganisms, typically by disrupting cell membranes. Examples include defensins, cathelicidins, and magainins.

### Analytical HPLC
High-Performance Liquid Chromatography used for purity determination and quality control. Analyzes microgram quantities, providing retention time, peak area, and purity percentage. See also: Preparative HPLC.

### Angiogenesis
The formation of new blood vessels from existing ones. Several peptides studied at RPL Peptide (including BPC-157 and GHK-Cu) are known to promote angiogenesis through VEGF upregulation.

### Anhydrous
Free from water. Used to describe solvents and storage conditions for moisture-sensitive peptides.

### Arrhenius Equation
A mathematical model (k = Ae^(-Ea/RT)) used to predict peptide degradation rates at different temperatures. Applied in accelerated stability studies to estimate shelf life at recommended storage conditions.

### Auto-Oxidation
Spontaneous oxidation of a peptide upon exposure to atmospheric oxygen. Most commonly affects methionine, cysteine, and tryptophan residues. Minimized by inert gas blanketing and antioxidant excipients.

---

## B

### β-Elimination
A degradation pathway involving the loss of a side chain and formation of a dehydroalanine residue. Affects Cys, Ser, Thr, and phosphorylated residues, particularly under basic conditions.

### β-Sheet
A secondary structure in which peptide strands align side-by-side, stabilized by hydrogen bonds between backbone amides. Can cause aggregation during SPPS, making some sequences difficult to synthesize.

### Batch (Lot)
A specific production run of a peptide, identified by a unique batch or lot number. Each batch is independently tested and a Certificate of Analysis is issued. Batch traceability is critical for research reproducibility.

### Biotinylation
The covalent attachment of biotin (vitamin B7) to a peptide. Biotinylated peptides are used in affinity purification, pull-down assays, and detection systems through biotin-streptavidin binding.

### Boc Chemistry
t-Butyloxycarbonyl (Boc) peptide synthesis — an older SPPS method using acid-labile Boc for N-terminal protection and HF for final cleavage. Largely superseded by Fmoc chemistry due to HF hazards. See also: Fmoc Chemistry.

### BPC-157
Body Protection Compound-157 — a 15-amino acid peptide derived from human gastric juice, studied for tissue repair, angiogenesis, and cytoprotection. Available at https://rplpeptides.com.

---

## C

### C-Terminus
The end of a peptide with a free carboxyl group (—COOH). In SPPS, synthesis proceeds from the C-terminus to the N-terminus.

### C18 Column
The standard reversed-phase HPLC column for peptide analysis, consisting of silica particles with 18-carbon alkyl chains bonded to the surface. Provides hydrophobic interactions for peptide separation.

### CAS Number
Chemical Abstracts Service Registry Number — a unique numerical identifier for every chemical substance. Essential for unambiguous identification of peptides in ordering, literature searching, and regulatory compliance.

### CD (Circular Dichroism)
A spectroscopic technique used to determine the secondary structure content (α-helix, β-sheet, random coil) of peptides in solution.

### Cleavage
The step in SPPS where the completed peptide is released from the solid resin support, typically using a TFA-based cocktail containing scavengers.

### COA (Certificate of Analysis)
A quality document reporting batch-specific analytical results including HPLC purity, MS identity confirmation, peptide content, moisture, and counter-ion analysis. Available for download at https://rplpeptides.com.

### Counter-Ion
An ion of opposite charge associated with the peptide to maintain electrical neutrality. The most common counter-ion in research peptides is TFA (trifluoroacetate) from HPLC purification. The counter-ion contributes to the total weight but not the biological activity. See our dedicated white paper at https://rplpeptides.com/white-papers/counter-ion-guide.

### Coupling
The chemical reaction that forms a peptide bond between two amino acids during SPPS. Requires activation of the carboxyl group of the incoming amino acid.

### Crude Peptide
The unpurified peptide product obtained after cleavage from the resin. Crude purity typically ranges from 30–80% and is purified to ≥98% by preparative HPLC.

### Cyclization
The formation of a ring structure in a peptide, either through disulfide bonds (between two Cys residues), amide bonds (head-to-tail), or other chemical linkages. Cyclization restricts conformational flexibility and often improves stability and receptor binding.

---

## D

### D-isomer
The mirror-image configuration of an L-amino acid. D-amino acids are sometimes incorporated into synthetic peptides to improve stability against enzymatic degradation. See also: Racemization.

### Deamidation
The non-enzymatic conversion of asparagine (Asn) to aspartic acid (Asp) or glutamine (Gln) to glutamic acid (Glu). A common degradation pathway, especially in solution at neutral to basic pH. Accelerated for Asn-Gly sequences.

### Deconvolution
The mathematical process of converting multi-charge mass spectrum data into the neutral molecular weight of the peptide. Essential for ESI-MS interpretation.

### Degradation
Any chemical or physical change that reduces the purity, activity, or quality of a peptide. Includes hydrolysis, oxidation, deamidation, aggregation, and racemization.

### Deprotection
In SPPS, the removal of a protecting group (typically Fmoc) to expose a reactive amine for the next coupling step.

### Desiccator
A sealed container used to store peptides and other moisture-sensitive materials with a desiccant (e.g., silica gel) to maintain low humidity.

### Dipeptide
A molecule composed of two amino acids linked by a single peptide bond.

### DKP (Diketopiperazine)
A cyclic compound formed from two amino acids, most commonly occurring as a side reaction during SPPS when the second amino acid is coupled.

### DLS (Dynamic Light Scattering)
A technique for measuring particle size distribution in peptide solutions, used to detect aggregates and monitor physical stability.

### DMF (Dimethylformamide)
The most common solvent for Fmoc SPPS, used for deprotection, coupling, and washing steps.

### DMSO (Dimethyl Sulfoxide)
A polar aprotic solvent sometimes used to dissolve hydrophobic peptides. Limit final concentration to <0.1% in cell-based assays.

### DPP-4 (Dipeptidyl Peptidase-4)
An enzyme that cleaves GLP-1 at the Ala-Glu bond (positions 2–3), rapidly inactivating the native hormone. GLP-1 analogs are modified at position 2 or 8 to resist DPP-4 cleavage.

### Dry Ice Shipping
Temperature-controlled shipping method using solid carbon dioxide (-78.5°C). Used for temperature-sensitive peptide shipments.

---

## E

### EC₅₀
Half-maximal effective concentration — the peptide concentration that produces 50% of the maximum response in a bioassay. Lower EC₅₀ indicates higher potency.

### Endotoxin
Lipopolysaccharide (LPS) components from Gram-negative bacterial cell walls. Endotoxin contamination is tested by the Limulus Amebocyte Lysate (LAL) assay. Research-grade peptides typically have <5 EU/mg.

### ESI-TOF MS
Electrospray Ionization Time-of-Flight Mass Spectrometry — the standard method for peptide molecular weight determination. Soft ionization produces intact peptide ions with high mass accuracy.

### Excipient
An inactive substance added to a peptide formulation to improve stability, solubility, or handling. Common excipients include trehalose, sucrose, mannitol, and BSA.

### Expiry Date
The date after which a product should not be used, based on stability data. See also: Retest Date.

---

## F

### Fluorescent Labeling
The attachment of a fluorescent dye (e.g., FITC, TAMRA, FAM) to a peptide for detection in fluorescence-based assays, microscopy, and flow cytometry.

### Fmoc Chemistry
9-Fluorenylmethoxycarbonyl — the dominant SPPS method using base-labile Fmoc for N-terminal protection. Deprotection with piperidine; final cleavage with TFA. Safer and more versatile than Boc chemistry.

### Forced Degradation
A stability study design where peptides are exposed to extreme conditions (high temperature, pH extremes, light, oxidants) to identify degradation pathways and characterize degradation products.

### Freeze-Thaw Cycle
The process of freezing and thawing a peptide solution. Each cycle causes some degradation; limit to maximum 3 cycles. Always aliquot to avoid repeated cycling.

### FTIR (Fourier-Transform Infrared Spectroscopy)
An analytical technique that can be used to assess peptide secondary structure and detect conformational changes.

---

## G

### GCGR (Glucagon Receptor)
The receptor for the hormone glucagon. Activation by triple agonists like Retatrutide adds energy expenditure pathways to GLP-1 and GIP effects.

### GHRH (Growth Hormone-Releasing Hormone)
A 44-amino acid hypothalamic peptide that stimulates the release of growth hormone from the pituitary.

### GHRP (Growth Hormone-Releasing Peptide)
Synthetic peptides that stimulate GH release through the ghrelin receptor (GHS-R1a). Examples: GHRP-2, GHRP-6, Ipamorelin.

### GIP (Glucose-Dependent Insulinotropic Polypeptide)
An incretin hormone that enhances insulin secretion. Dual GIP/GLP-1 agonists like Tirzepatide target both receptors.

### GLP-1 (Glucagon-Like Peptide-1)
A 30-31 amino acid incretin hormone that stimulates glucose-dependent insulin secretion. Native GLP-1 has a 1-2 minute half-life. Synthetic analogs are modified for extended half-life.

### GPCR (G Protein-Coupled Receptor)
A large family of cell surface receptors, including receptors for many peptides (GLP-1R, GIP-R, GCGR, GHS-R1a). The primary target class for peptide therapeutics.

### Gross Weight
The total mass of the vial contents including peptide, counter-ion, and residual moisture. See also: Peptide Content.

---

## H

### Half-Life (t₁/₂)
The time required for the concentration or activity of a substance to decrease by half. In peptide research, refers to either chemical stability (degradation half-life) or biological half-life (clearance from circulation).

### HATU
A coupling reagent (1-[Bis(dimethylamino)methylene]-1H-1,2,3-triazolo[4,5-b]pyridinium 3-oxid hexafluorophosphate) used for difficult peptide couplings.

### HBTU
A standard coupling reagent (2-(1H-Benzotriazole-1-yl)-1,1,3,3-tetramethyluronium hexafluorophosphate) widely used in Fmoc SPPS.

### HOBt (Hydroxybenzotriazole)
An additive used in peptide coupling reactions to suppress racemization and improve coupling efficiency.

### HPLC (High-Performance Liquid Chromatography)
The primary analytical method for determining peptide purity. Separates peptide components by hydrophobicity using a C18 column and UV detection at 214 nm.

### Hydrolysis
The cleavage of a peptide bond by water, most common at Asp-Pro and Asp-Gly sequences. Accelerated by acidic or basic conditions and elevated temperature.

### Hydrophobicity
The tendency of a peptide or amino acid to avoid water. Hydrophobic peptides (rich in Leu, Ile, Val, Phe, Trp) may be poorly water-soluble and require special handling.

---

## I

### Identity Confirmation
The process of verifying that a peptide is the intended product, typically by matching HPLC retention time and confirming molecular weight by MS.

### Impurity
Any substance in a peptide sample other than the target peptide. May include truncated sequences, deletion sequences, oxidation products, reagents, or solvents.

### In Vivo
Latin for "within the living." Research conducted in live organisms. Peptides for in vivo studies require higher purity (≥98%) and endotoxin testing.

### In Vitro
Latin for "within the glass." Research conducted in controlled laboratory conditions (cell culture, biochemical assays).

### Incretin
A gut hormone that stimulates insulin secretion in response to food intake. The two main incretins are GLP-1 and GIP.

### IP (Intellectual Property)
Patent and legal protections that may apply to peptide sequences, modifications, and uses. Researchers should verify freedom-to-operate before commercial applications.

### Isoelectric Point (pI)
The pH at which a peptide carries no net electrical charge. Peptides are least soluble near their pI and more soluble at pH values significantly above or below it.

---

## K

### Kaiser Test (Ninhydrin Test)
A colorimetric test used during SPPS to detect the presence of free amines. A blue-purple color indicates incomplete coupling; colorless indicates complete reaction.

### Karl Fischer Titration
A method for determining water content in peptide samples. Critical for proper dosage and stability assessment. Specifications typically require <5% moisture.

---

## L

### LAL (Limulus Amebocyte Lysate)
A test for endotoxin contamination using blood cells from the horseshoe crab. Results reported in EU/mg (endotoxin units per milligram).

### LC-MS
Liquid Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry — a hyphenated technique combining HPLC separation with MS detection. Provides both purity and identity data in a single analysis.

### Loading (Resin)
The amount of first amino acid attached to the resin, expressed in mmol/g. Higher loading yields more peptide per gram of resin but may reduce coupling efficiency for difficult sequences.

### Lyophilization (Freeze-Drying)
The process of removing water from a frozen peptide solution under vacuum, producing a stable, porous powder. Essential for long-term peptide stability.

---

## M

### Mass Accuracy
The precision of molecular weight measurement by MS. ±0.5 Da is standard for ESI-TOF MS peptide identity confirmation.

### MicroBatch (µBatch)
A small-scale peptide production batch, typically 1–50 mg, for research and screening applications.

### Monoisotopic Mass
The exact mass of a molecule calculated using the mass of the most abundant isotope of each element. Used for MS identification. Distinguished from average mass which accounts for natural isotopic distribution.

### MW (Molecular Weight)
The mass of a peptide molecule, expressed in Daltons (Da). For peptides, reported as the monoisotopic mass of the free base form. The salt form (with counter-ion) will have a higher mass.

---

## N

### N-Terminus
The end of a peptide with a free amino group (—NH₂ or —NHR). In SPPS, the N-terminus of the growing chain is deprotected before each coupling.

### Net Peptide Weight
The mass of the peptide alone, excluding counter-ion and moisture. Calculated as gross weight × peptide content percentage. Used for accurate concentration calculations.

### NMR (Nuclear Magnetic Resonance)
A structural biology technique that can determine the three-dimensional structure of peptides in solution.

---

## O

### Orthogonal Protection
A protection strategy in SPPS where different protecting groups are removed by different chemical mechanisms (e.g., Fmoc removed by base, side-chain protecting groups by acid).

### Oxidation
The addition of oxygen atoms to a peptide, most commonly at methionine (Met→MetO), cysteine (Cys→cysteic acid), and tryptophan residues. A major degradation pathway, accelerated by light, heat, and metal ions.

---

## P

### Peptide Bond
The covalent amide bond (—CO—NH—) linking amino acids in a peptide or protein. Formed between the carboxyl group of one amino acid and the amino group of another.

### Peptide Content
The percentage of the total vial weight that is actual peptide, excluding counter-ion and moisture. Reported on the COA. Essential for accurate concentration calculations.

### Peptide Mapping
An analytical technique where a peptide is digested with a specific protease (e.g., trypsin) and the fragments are analyzed by LC-MS to confirm the sequence and identify modifications.

### pH
A measure of acidity or basicity (0–14). Peptide stability and solubility are strongly pH-dependent. Most peptides are most stable at pH 4–6.

### Piperidine
A secondary amine (20% in DMF) used to remove the Fmoc protecting group during SPPS.

### PLGA (Poly(Lactic-co-Glycolic Acid))
A biodegradable polymer used in sustained-release peptide formulations (e.g., Exenatide LAR microspheres).

### Preparative HPLC
Large-scale HPLC used to purify peptides from crude synthesis material. Capable of processing milligram to kilogram quantities.

### Protecting Group
A chemical group temporarily attached to a reactive site to prevent unwanted reactions during synthesis. See also: Fmoc, Boc, Trt, Pbf, tBu.

### Purity
The percentage of the target peptide in the sample, measured by HPLC area % at 214 nm. RPL Peptide standard: ≥98%.

### Pyroglutamate (pGlu)
A cyclic form of glutamine (Gln) or glutamic acid (Glu) at the N-terminus. Forms spontaneously in solution, especially at neutral to basic pH.

---

## Q

### QC (Quality Control)
The systematic testing and documentation of peptide products to ensure they meet specifications. Includes HPLC, MS, content analysis, moisture testing, and endotoxin testing.

### QR Code
A scannable code printed on RPL Peptide vial labels linking directly to the digital Certificate of Analysis at https://rplpeptides.com.

---

## R

### Racemization
The conversion of an L-amino acid to its D-isomer during synthesis or storage. Most common at aspartic acid (Asp) residues. Suppressed by using HOBt or Oxyma during coupling.

### Reconstitution
The process of dissolving lyophilized peptide in an appropriate solvent to prepare a solution for experimental use.

### Resin
Insoluble polymer beads used as the solid support in SPPS. Common types include Wang, Rink Amide, and CTC resins.

### Retention Time (RT)
The time a peptide takes to elute from an HPLC column. Used as a preliminary identification parameter. Characteristic for each peptide under defined conditions.

### Retest Date
The date until which the product is expected to remain within specifications under recommended storage conditions. See also: Expiry Date.

### RP-HPLC (Reversed-Phase HPLC)
The standard HPLC mode for peptide analysis, using a non-polar stationary phase (C18) and a polar mobile phase (water-acetonitrile with 0.1% TFA).

### RPL Peptide
Qingdao RPL Biotechnology Co., Ltd. — manufacturer and supplier of research-grade peptides. Website: https://rplpeptides.com.

---

## S

### Scavenger
A compound added to TFA cleavage cocktails to capture reactive carbocations and prevent side reactions. Common scavengers: TIS, EDT, phenol, water, thioanisole.

### SEC (Size-Exclusion Chromatography)
A chromatography method that separates peptide aggregates from monomers based on molecular size. Used to detect and quantify aggregation.

### Sequence
The order of amino acids in a peptide, written from N-terminus (left) to C-terminus (right). The sequence determines the peptide's structure and biological activity.

### Shelf Life
The period during which a product is expected to remain within its quality specifications under recommended storage conditions.

### SPPS (Solid-Phase Peptide Synthesis)
The standard method for chemical synthesis of peptides, where the growing chain is attached to insoluble resin beads. See our dedicated white paper at https://rplpeptides.com/white-papers/peptide-synthesis-methods.

### Stability
The ability of a peptide to maintain its chemical and physical properties over time under specified storage conditions.

---

## T

### TDS (Technical Data Sheet)
A document summarizing the physical and chemical properties of a peptide product, including molecular formula, CAS number, appearance, and storage recommendations.

### TFA (Trifluoroacetic Acid)
An acid used in HPLC mobile phases (0.1% as ion-pairing agent) and in cleavage cocktails (95%). TFA is the most common counter-ion in research-grade peptides.

### TIS (Triisopropylsilane)
A carbocation scavenger used in TFA cleavage cocktails.

### Trt (Trityl)
A protecting group for the side chains of Asn, Gln, His, and Cys. Removed by TFA during final cleavage.

### Truncation
A synthesis error where the peptide chain terminates early, producing shorter peptides. Minimized by careful monitoring and optimized coupling conditions.

---

## U

### UV Detection
The primary detection method in HPLC peptide analysis. 214 nm detects the peptide bond; 280 nm detects aromatic amino acids (Trp, Tyr).

---

## V

### VEGF (Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor)
A signaling protein that stimulates angiogenesis. Upregulated by several peptides including BPC-157 and GHK-Cu.

### Vortex
A mixing method that should NOT be used for peptide solutions, as it can cause aggregation, foaming, and degradation. Use gentle swirling instead.

---

## W

### Wang Resin
A standard SPPS resin producing peptides with C-terminal carboxylic acids. Cleavage with TFA (≥95%).

### Water-for-Injection (WFI)
High-purity water used for peptide reconstitution. Sterile, endotoxin-free, and particle-free.

---

## X

### X-Ray Crystallography
A structural biology technique that can determine peptide three-dimensional structures at atomic resolution, often in complex with target proteins.

---

## Y

### Yield
The amount of purified peptide obtained relative to the theoretical maximum. Typical yields: crude ~90%; purified ~30–60% of crude.

---

## Z

### z-Score
A statistical measure used in MS data quality assessment, indicating the confidence of charge state assignment in ESI-MS deconvolution.
