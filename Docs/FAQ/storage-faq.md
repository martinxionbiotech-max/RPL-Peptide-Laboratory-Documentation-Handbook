## 1. Storage Conditions

### Q1: What should I do immediately upon receiving a peptide shipment?
**A:** Follow the receiving checklist: (1) Inspect the package for damage — tears, punctures, water damage, or crushed corners. (2) Check temperature indicators — gel packs should still be cold or dry ice present. (3) Verify product labels match the packing slip and your purchase order. (4) Inspect the vial for cracks, broken seals, or missing flip-off caps. (5) Confirm the batch number matches the Certificate of Analysis. (6) Count the quantity. If all checks pass, store immediately at the recommended temperature. If there is a temperature excursion, visual damage, or mismatch, document the issue with photos and contact RPL Peptide customer service.

### Q2: Where should I store lyophilized peptides for optimal stability?
**A:** Lyophilized (freeze-dried) peptides should be stored at -20°C in a manual-defrost laboratory freezer. For long-term archival storage (2+ years), -80°C ultra-low freezers provide superior stability. Avoid frost-free freezers because their defrost cycles cause temperature swings above 0°C, which can damage the lyophilized cake. Store vials in sealed, desiccated containers inside the freezer to protect against moisture condensation when the freezer door is opened. Always keep vials in their original packaging or a secondary light-protective container.

### Q3: How long can lyophilized peptide be stored at 4°C (refrigerator)?
**A:** Lyophilized peptide stored at 4°C in a sealed, desiccated vial is generally stable for up to 12 months. However, at 4°C the degradation rate is 10–50 times higher than at -20°C, so this is only recommended if a -20°C freezer is unavailable or if the peptide will be used within a year. For long-term banking of rare or expensive peptides, always use -20°C or -80°C. If storing at 4°C, use desiccant in the storage container and minimize exposure to ambient humidity when accessing the vial.

### Q4: Should I worry about light sensitivity for all peptides?
**A:** Peptides containing tryptophan, tyrosine, methionine, or cysteine residues are particularly light-sensitive because these side chains undergo photo-oxidation when exposed to UV and visible light. However, as a general precaution, all peptides should be protected from prolonged light exposure. Store vials in their original opaque or amber packaging, or place clear vials in a drawer or light-proof container. If you transfer a peptide to a new tube or aliquot it, use amber or foil-wrapped tubes. Fluorescent lab lighting can cause measurable photo-degradation over extended periods.

### Q5: What role do desiccants play in peptide storage?
**A:** Desiccants (silica gel, molecular sieves, or calcium sulfate) absorb moisture from the air inside the storage container. Lyophilized peptides are hygroscopic — they readily absorb water vapor, which can cause the lyophilized cake to collapse, accelerate hydrolysis, promote aggregation, and support microbial growth. A color-change desiccant (e.g., blue-indicating silica gel that turns pink when saturated) provides a visual signal that it needs regeneration or replacement. The storage container should be opened as infrequently as possible to maintain low relative humidity.

### Q6: How does temperature affect the shelf life of lyophilized versus reconstituted peptides?
**A:** Temperature has a dramatically different impact on each form. For lyophilized peptides: -20°C provides 2+ years shelf life, while room temperature (25°C) reduces this to approximately 30 days. For reconstituted peptides: 4°C provides up to 30 days, -20°C (properly aliquoted) provides up to 6 months, and room temperature is not recommended at all. The degradation rate of reconstituted peptide is orders of magnitude faster than lyophilized because water is available to participate in hydrolysis reactions and accelerates other degradation pathways.

## 2. Handling Best Practices

### Q7: Why must I allow the vial to reach room temperature before opening?
**A:** When a vial is removed from -20°C or -80°C storage, the glass and contents are well below the dew point of ambient air. Opening the vial immediately would cause moisture from the air to condense onto the cold vial interior and the lyophilized cake. This condensation introduces water into the product, compromising stability and potentially dissolving the peptide. Always allow the sealed vial to equilibrate to room temperature — typically 15–30 minutes depending on vial size — in a desiccated environment before opening.

### Q8: What is the proper technique for reconstituting a lyophilized peptide?
**A:** (1) Allow the sealed vial to reach room temperature (to prevent condensation). (2) Prepare the solvent — sterile water-for-injection is suitable for most applications; PBS or 0.9% saline for biological assays. (3) Gently add the solvent down the inner wall of the vial using a sterile syringe and needle. (4) Swirl gently or invert the vial slowly — do not vortex or shake vigorously, as this causes foaming and aggregation. (5) Allow the solution to rest for 5–10 minutes at room temperature for complete dissolution. (6) Inspect visually for any undissolved material or particulate matter. If using for cell culture, filter through a 0.22 μm sterile filter.

### Q9: What is the best way to aliquot reconstituted peptide to avoid freeze-thaw damage?
**A:** (1) Calculate the volume needed for a single experiment. (2) Pre-label low-binding microcentrifuge tubes with the peptide name, concentration, date, and lot number. (3) Dispense the calculated volume into each tube. (4) For optimal preservation, flash-freeze the aliquots in liquid nitrogen before transferring to -20°C or -80°C storage. (5) When needed, thaw a single aliquot on ice or at 4°C — never use a heating block or warm water bath. (6) Use the aliquot immediately and discard any unused portion. Never refreeze a thawed aliquot.

### Q10: How should I label my peptide vials and aliquots?
**A:** Vial labels should include: peptide name, batch/lot number, declared content (e.g., 5 mg), purity, storage temperature, manufacturing date, and expiry/retest date. Aliquot labels should include: peptide name, concentration (e.g., 5 mM or 5 mg/mL), lot number, preparation date, and the user's initials. Use labels designed for cold storage (cryogenic-rated adhesive) that will not peel off in the freezer. Always use a waterproof marker or printed label. The original vial label should never be removed or obscured.

### Q11: Can I store multiple different peptides together in the same freezer box?
**A:** Yes, as long as all vials are properly sealed and clearly labeled. Store them in a well-organized box with dividers to prevent vials from contacting each other. Group related peptides together and maintain a digital or paper inventory that records the box location, peptide name, lot number, and date placed in storage. Keep an inventory log near the freezer. Most importantly, ensure that every vial is tightly sealed to prevent cross-contamination between products — especially if opening vials inside the freezer storage area.

### Q12: What should I do if my peptide shipment arrives at room temperature when it requires cold storage?
**A:** If the temperature indicator shows an excursion, first document the condition — take photos of the indicator and package, record the temperature and duration if a data logger was included. Store the peptide immediately at the recommended temperature. Contact RPL Peptide's customer service or quality team with the batch number and excursion details. A short excursion (<2 hours at 25°C) may be acceptable — assess visually and store normally. For moderate excursions (2–24 hours), we recommend reanalysis (HPLC) before use. For severe excursions (>24 hours above 25°C or above 40°C at any duration), do not use the product and contact the supplier for replacement.
