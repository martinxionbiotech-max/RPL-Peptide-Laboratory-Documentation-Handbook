# Peptide

## Definition

A peptide is a short chain of amino acids linked by peptide bonds, typically containing 2–50 amino acid residues. Peptides are smaller than proteins and serve as critical research tools in biomedical and pharmaceutical studies.

## Overview

Peptides are fundamental molecules in biological systems, functioning as hormones, neurotransmitters, growth factors, and signaling molecules. Synthetic peptides — manufactured primarily through Solid Phase Peptide Synthesis (SPPS) — allow researchers to study specific biological mechanisms, develop therapeutic candidates, and conduct controlled experiments.

## Technical Explanation

**Peptide Structure:** A peptide consists of amino acids joined by peptide bonds (amide bonds) formed between the carboxyl group of one amino acid and the amino group of the next.

**Classification by Length:**
- Dipeptide: 2 amino acids
- Oligopeptide: ~2–20 amino acids
- Polypeptide: ~20–50 amino acids
- Protein: >50 amino acids (though boundaries are flexible)

**Synthetic Peptides:**
Manufactured through SPPS, where amino acids are sequentially coupled to a solid resin support. After synthesis, peptides are cleaved, purified (typically by HPLC), and lyophilized into a stable powder form.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What is the difference between a peptide and a protein?**
A: The distinction is primarily size — peptides are shorter chains (typically <50 amino acids), while proteins are longer and usually fold into complex tertiary structures. There is no universally fixed cutoff.

**Q: Are all research peptides synthetic?**
A: Most research-grade peptides are synthetic (produced via SPPS), but some may be produced through recombinant methods. RPL Peptide products are synthetically manufactured for consistent quality and purity.
