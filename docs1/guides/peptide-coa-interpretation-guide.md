## 1. Introduction

The Certificate of Analysis (COA) is the primary quality document for each peptide batch. Proper interpretation ensures you understand the product you are using.

## 2. What is a COA?

A quality document reporting actual measured values for a specific production batch. Each COA is unique to its batch and includes all analytical test results.

## 3. COA Structure

Standard COA sections:
1. Header (product, batch, dates)
2. Appearance result
3. Identity (HPLC RT + MS)
4. Purity (HPLC area %)
5. Peptide content (%)
6. Counter-ion content
7. Water content
8. Endotoxin (if applicable)

## 4. Batch Number

Unique identifier for each production run.
Format: [Product]-[Year]-[Sequence]
Used for traceability and COA lookup.

## 5. Identity

Confirmed by two methods:
1. HPLC retention time match (±0.5 min)
2. Mass spectrometry (±0.5 Da accuracy)
Both must match for identity confirmation.

## 6. Purity

HPLC area % at 214 nm.
≥98% = Standard grade
≥99% = High purity grade
Calculated as: (Main peak area / Total area) × 100

## 7. Water Content

Measured by Karl Fischer titration.
Specification: <5%.
Higher moisture content may affect stability.

## 8. Counter-ion

Typically TFA (trifluoroacetate) from HPLC purification.
Reported as weight % of the total sample.
Affects peptide content calculation.

## 9. Residual Solvents

Tested by GC headspace analysis when applicable.
Specifications follow ICH guidelines.

## 10. HPLC Results

The HPLC chromatogram shows:
- Main peak (target peptide)
- Impurity peaks
- Retention time
- Peak symmetry
- Resolution from impurities

## 11. MS Results

The mass spectrum shows:
- Charge state envelope
- Deconvoluted molecular weight
- Match to theoretical mass (±0.5 Da)

## 12. Acceptance Criteria

| Test | Specification |
|:-----|:-------------|
| Appearance | White to off-white powder |
| Identity | Conforms by HPLC + MS |
| Purity | ≥98% |
| Water | <5% |

## 13. Common Questions

Q: What if COA purity is 97.8%?
A: Slightly below 98% — contact RPL Peptide for discussion.

Q: Can I compare COAs from different batches?
A: Yes, but minor variations are normal.
