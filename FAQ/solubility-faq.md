## 1. General Solubility

### Q1: What is the best solvent for dissolving peptides?
**A:** Sterile water-for-injection or PBS (pH 7.4) is suitable for most peptides. For peptides with poor aqueous solubility, alternatives include 0.1–1% acetic acid, dilute ammonium hydroxide, or DMSO (for stock solutions). The recommended solvent for each product is listed on its product page at https://rplpeptides.com.

### Q2: How do solvent choice and pH affect solubility?
**A:** Peptide solubility is strongly influenced by pH. Peptides are least soluble near their isoelectric point (pI) and more soluble at pH values significantly above or below the pI. Basic peptides (Lys, Arg-rich) dissolve better in acidic solutions. Acidic peptides (Glu, Asp-rich) dissolve better in neutral to basic solutions. For detailed guidance, see our Reconstitution Technical Guide at https://rplpeptides.com/white-papers/reconstitution-technical-guide.

### Q3: What if my peptide does not dissolve?
**A:** Try the following in order:
1. Allow more time (some peptides take 15–30 minutes to fully dissolve)
2. Gently warm to 30°C (water bath, 5 minutes)
3. Add more solvent (dilute 2–3×)
4. Adjust pH (±1 unit from the current value)
5. Use a small amount of DMSO or dilute acetic acid
6. Contact technical support at https://rplpeptides.com/contact

## 2. Solvent Selection

### Q4: Can I use DMSO to reconstitute my peptide?
**A:** Yes, DMSO is an excellent solvent for peptide stock solutions. However, DMSO can be cytotoxic at concentrations >0.1% in cell-based assays. For in vivo work, avoid DMSO unless specifically required. If using DMSO, prepare a concentrated stock and dilute into your final buffer.

### Q5: What concentration should I aim for?
**A:** A stock concentration of 1–10 mg/mL is typical for most research peptides. Some hydrophobic peptides may not exceed 1 mg/mL. Very soluble peptides can reach 20–50 mg/mL. Start with a moderate concentration and increase if needed.

### Q6: Do peptides contain salt that affects solubility?
**A:** Yes. Peptides purified by HPLC typically contain TFA as a counter-ion, which can affect solubility. The TFA salt form is generally well-soluble in aqueous solutions. For acetate salt forms (available on request), solubility may differ slightly.

## 3. Troubleshooting

### Q7: Why did my peptide solution become cloudy?
**A:** Cloudiness indicates precipitation or aggregation. Possible causes:
- The peptide concentration exceeded its solubility limit
- The pH shifted during storage
- Temperature fluctuations
- Contamination
- Dilute by 2× and re-evaluate

### Q8: What is the "gel" I see in my reconstituted peptide?
**A:** Some peptides (especially concentrated solutions) can form a gel-like consistency. This is typically a result of peptide-peptide interactions at high concentration. Dilution with gentle mixing usually resolves the gel.

### Q9: Should I filter my peptide solution?
**A:** For cell culture and in vivo work, filter through a sterile 0.22 μm syringe filter. For chemical assays and analytical work, filtration is optional. Note that some peptide may adsorb to the filter membrane; pre-wet the filter with buffer to minimize loss.
