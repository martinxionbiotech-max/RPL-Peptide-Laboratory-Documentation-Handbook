## 1. General Information

### Q1: What is mass spectrometry and why is it used for peptide analysis?
**A:** Mass spectrometry (MS) is an analytical technique that measures the mass-to-charge ratio (m/z) of ions. For peptides, MS provides precise molecular weight determination, confirming the identity of the synthesized peptide and detecting impurities not visible by HPLC. All RPL Peptide COAs include MS identity confirmation.

### Q2: What type of mass spectrometry do you use?
**A:** We use Electrospray Ionization Time-of-Flight Mass Spectrometry (ESI-TOF MS), which provides high mass accuracy (±0.5 Da) and is the standard method for peptide analysis. ESI is a "soft" ionization technique that preserves the intact peptide molecule without fragmentation.

### Q3: Why do I see multiple peaks in the mass spectrum?
**A:** Peptides in ESI-MS typically form multiple charge states (protonated species), producing a series of peaks at different m/z values. For example, a 4000 Da peptide may appear as [M+3H]³⁺ at m/z 1334, [M+4H]⁴⁺ at m/z 1001, and [M+5H]⁵⁺ at m/z 801. Each represents the same peptide with different charge states.

## 2. Interpreting MS Data

### Q4: How do I calculate the molecular weight from the mass spectrum?
**A:** Multiply each m/z value by its charge state and subtract the mass of the added protons. Deconvolution software automatically performs this calculation, displaying the neutral molecular weight. The reported molecular weight on your COA is the deconvoluted monoisotopic mass.

### Q5: What mass accuracy is acceptable for identity confirmation?
**A:** We consider ±0.5 Da acceptable for identity confirmation with ESI-TOF MS. This tolerance accounts for instrument calibration, sample preparation, and the natural isotopic distribution of the peptide.

### Q6: What does it mean if I see a peak at [M+16] or [M+32]?
**A:** These peaks typically indicate oxidation, where one (+16) or two (+32) oxygen atoms have been added to the peptide. Methionine (Met), cysteine (Cys), and tryptophan (Trp) residues are most susceptible to oxidation. Some oxidation is normal and does not necessarily indicate poor quality.

### Q7: What does a peak at [M-18] or [M-17] indicate?
**A:** [M-18] (loss of H₂O) or [M-17] (loss of NH₃) may indicate deamidation or pyroglutamate formation. These can occur naturally over time or during ionization.

---

## 3. Quality Assurance

### Q8: Do you use MS for quantification?
**A:** We use HPLC (UV detection at 214 nm) for quantification and purity determination. MS is used for identity confirmation, not quantification, because ionization efficiency varies between compounds and MS signal intensity does not directly correlate with concentration.

### Q9: What is LC-MS versus direct infusion MS?
**A:** LC-MS couples HPLC separation with MS detection, allowing individual peaks from the chromatogram to be identified by their mass. Direct infusion MS injects the sample directly into the mass spectrometer without prior separation. We use LC-MS for COA analysis to confirm the identity of the main peak and identify any impurity peaks.

### Q10: Why does my own MS analysis show a different mass?
**A:** Possible reasons include:
- Different calibration standards
- Different instrument type or settings
- Degradation during storage or handling
- Use of the average mass instead of monoisotopic mass
- Salt adducts (Na⁺, K⁺)

Contact us at https://rplpeptides.com/contact if you suspect a mass discrepancy.
