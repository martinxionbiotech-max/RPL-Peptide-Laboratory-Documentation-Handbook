## 1. General Information

### Q1: What is HPLC and why is it used for peptide analysis?
**A:** High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) is the standard analytical method for determining peptide purity. It separates components of a sample based on their chemical properties, allowing quantification of the target peptide and detection of impurities. All RPL Peptide products are analyzed by RP-HPLC (Reversed-Phase HPLC) with UV detection at 214 nm. For a comprehensive guide, see our white paper at https://rplpeptides.com/white-papers/how-to-read-hplc.

### Q2: Why is detection at 214 nm used?
**A:** Peptide bonds absorb UV light strongly at approximately 214 nm. This wavelength provides the most universal and sensitive detection for peptides, as every peptide bond contributes to the signal. Detection at 280 nm (aromatic amino acids) is less sensitive and sequence-dependent.

### Q3: What does the HPLC chromatogram tell me?
**A:** The chromatogram shows:
- **Main peak:** The target peptide — retention time and area percentage
- **Impurity peaks:** Any byproducts, degradation products, or synthesis-related impurities
- **Peak shape:** Sharp, symmetrical peaks indicate good column performance and sample quality

## 2. Interpreting HPLC Data

### Q4: What is retention time (RT)?
**A:** Retention time is the time it takes for a compound to travel from injection to detection. It is used as a preliminary identification parameter. Each peptide has a characteristic RT under specified HPLC conditions. The RT on the COA should be considered a reference; column aging and system variations can cause minor shifts.

### Q5: What is HPLC purity (area %)?
**A:** HPLC purity is calculated as:
```
Purity (%) = (Area of main peak / Total integrated area) × 100
```
This represents the relative purity by UV absorption, not absolute mass purity. A value of ≥98% is our standard specification.

### Q6: What might small peaks near the main peak indicate?
**A:** Peaks eluting very close to the main peak may indicate:
- **Oxidation products** (earlier-eluting)
- **Deamidation products** (earlier-eluting)
- **Isomeric impurities** (may elute before or after)
- **Truncated sequences** (usually earlier-eluting)

## 3. Method Details

### Q7: What HPLC column is typically used?
**A:** We use C18 reversed-phase columns (silica-based, 5 μm particle size, typically 4.6 × 250 mm) with a gradient of water-acetonitrile containing 0.1% TFA as the ion-pairing agent. This is the standard method for peptide analysis in the industry.

### Q8: What is the gradient program?
**A:** A typical gradient runs from 20% to 60% acetonitrile (with 0.1% TFA) over 20–30 minutes at 1.0 mL/min. The exact program is optimized for each peptide and is available on request.

### Q9: Can I reproduce your HPLC method in my lab?
**A:** Yes. The HPLC method conditions (column type, gradient, flow rate, detection wavelength) are provided on the COA. If you need further method details, contact us at https://rplpeptides.com/contact.

## 4. Quality Standards

### Q10: What is the difference between analytical and preparative HPLC?
**A:** Analytical HPLC (used for COA data) separates microgram quantities for analysis and purity determination. Preparative HPLC processes milligram to gram quantities for purification. Both use the same separation principle but at different scales.

### Q11: How does HPLC purity relate to biological activity?
**A:** HPLC purity is a chemical measurement and does not directly measure biological activity. However, higher purity generally correlates with more consistent and reliable research results because there are fewer confounding impurities.
