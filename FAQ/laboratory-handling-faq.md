## 1. Safety and Precautions

### Q1: What safety precautions should I take when handling peptides?
**A:** Standard laboratory safety practices apply:
- Wear appropriate PPE (lab coat, gloves, safety glasses)
- Work in a fume hood when handling lyophilized powder (to avoid inhalation)
- Use sterile technique for reconstitution
- Follow your institution's biosafety guidelines
- Treat all peptides as potentially bioactive
- Refer to the Safety Data Sheet (SDS) for each product at https://rplpeptides.com

### Q2: Are peptides hazardous?
**A:** Peptides are biochemical reagents and should be handled with care. Some peptides may have potent biological activity. Always follow safe laboratory practices. SDS documents are available upon request through https://rplpeptides.com/contact.

### Q3: How should I dispose of unused peptide?
**A:** Dispose of unused peptide and peptide-contaminated materials according to your institutional guidelines for biochemical waste. Do not dispose of peptides in regular trash or down the drain.

## 2. Equipment and Tools

### Q4: What equipment do I need to handle peptides?
**A:** Basic equipment includes:
- Precision analytical balance
- Microcentrifuge tubes (polypropylene, low-binding preferred)
- Adjustable micropipettes with sterile tips
- P2, P20, P200, P1000 pipettes for accurate volume measurement
- Vortex mixer (for buffer preparation only, not for peptide)
- Refrigerator (4°C) and freezer (-20°C or -80°C)

### Q5: What type of pipette tips should I use?
**A:** Use sterile, low-retention pipette tips for maximum accuracy, especially at low volumes (<10 μL). Standard tips are acceptable for larger volumes and stock solutions.

### Q6: Can I reuse tubes after cleaning?
**A:** Not recommended for peptide storage. Residual detergent, organic solvents, or adsorbed peptide can contaminate fresh samples. Use new, sterile tubes whenever possible.

---

## 3. Contamination Prevention

### Q7: How do I prevent contamination?
**A:** Key practices:
- Always use sterile technique
- Work in a clean area (laminar flow hood for sensitive applications)
- Use sterile, endotoxin-free water for reconstitution
- Do not touch the vial septum or stopper
- Recap vials promptly after use

### Q8: What if I accidentally introduce contamination?
**A:** If contamination is suspected, discard the affected solution. Do not attempt to salvage contaminated peptide — microbial growth will rapidly degrade the peptide and may introduce bioactive contaminants.

### Q9: How do I know if my peptide is contaminated?
**A:** Signs of contamination include:
- Cloudy solution after reconstitution
- Unusual odor
- Visible microbial growth (in solution)
- Unexpected HPLC peaks on re-analysis
