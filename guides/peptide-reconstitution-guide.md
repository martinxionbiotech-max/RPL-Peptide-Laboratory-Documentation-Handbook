## 1. Introduction

Reconstitution is the process of dissolving lyophilized peptide in an appropriate solvent. Proper technique ensures accurate concentration and maintains peptide integrity.

## 2. Before Reconstitution

[ ] Verify product against COA
[ ] Check batch number
[ ] Allow vial to reach room temperature (15–20 min)
[ ] Prepare sterile solvent
[ ] Calculate required volume

## 3. Selecting a Solvent

Water → General purpose
PBS → Cell culture, biological assays
0.1% AcOH → Hydrophobic peptides
DMSO → Stock solutions (for cell: <0.1% final)
See Solubility Guide for details.

## 4. Sterile Handling

Work in laminar flow hood when possible
Use sterile syringe and needle
Use sterile, endotoxin-free water
Avoid touching vial septum

## 5. Reconstitution Procedure

Step 1: Equilibrate vial to room temp (15–20 min)
Step 2: Calculate volume (see below)
Step 3: Inject solvent down vial wall
Step 4: Wait 2–5 min for initial wetting
Step 5: Swirl gently — do NOT vortex
Step 6: Wait 5–10 min for full dissolution
Step 7: Inspect for clarity

## 6. Mixing Techniques

✅ Gentle swirling
✅ Inverting the vial
✅ Brief bath sonication (low power, 30 sec)
❌ Vortexing
❌ Vigorous shaking

## 7. Aliquoting

Always aliquot before freezing:
1. Calculate single-use volume
2. Dispense into sterile tubes
3. Label with peptide, concentration, date
4. Flash freeze in liquid N₂ if possible
5. Store at -20°C

## 8. Storage After Reconstitution

Short-term (daily use): 4°C
Long-term (stock): -20°C (aliquoted)
Maximum storage at 4°C: 30 days
Maximum freeze-thaw cycles: 3

## 9. Freeze–Thaw Management

Each freeze-thaw cycle causes degradation. Best practice: prepare enough aliquots for single use. Never refreeze a thawed aliquot.

## 10. Common Mistakes

❌ Vortexing → aggregation
❌ Opening cold vial → moisture condensation
❌ Incorrect volume → wrong concentration
❌ Re-freezing stock → cumulative degradation

## 11. Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How long does dissolution take? A: 5–15 min typically.
Q: My peptide foamed. A: Let it settle; avoid vigorous mixing.
Q: Can I sonicate? A: Brief bath sonication only.
